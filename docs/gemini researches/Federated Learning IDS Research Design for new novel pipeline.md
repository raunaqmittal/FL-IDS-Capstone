# **Federated Learning-based Intrusion Detection Systems for IoT: Architecture, Novelty, and Defense Pipelines**

## **Deep Technical Analysis of Supplied Literature**

The intersection of Federated Learning (FL), Intrusion Detection Systems (IDS), and Internet of Things (IoT) edge networks presents a complex architectural challenge, primarily due to the severe constraints of edge devices and the inherent heterogeneity of IoT traffic. An exhaustive analysis of the contemporary literature surrounding Byzantine-robust FL provides critical insights into the mathematical formulations, aggregation logic, and anomaly detection mechanisms that define the current state-of-the-art.  
The Divide-and-Conquer (DnC) framework tackles untargeted model poisoning by leveraging spectral analysis and dimensionality reduction. The architecture functions entirely on the server-side, relying on Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) to identify the principal components of submitted client updates. The mathematical formulation filters updates by projecting them onto the principal eigenvector of the update matrix, operating on the assumption that malicious updates will maximize their variance along a specific, identifiable malicious direction. The anomaly detection mechanism isolates and removes a constant fraction of model updates that exhibit the largest scalar projections on this principal component. While DnC demonstrates formidable strengths against untargeted attacks in environments where benign data is Identically and Independently Distributed (IID), its weaknesses become apparent in highly Non-IID settings. Furthermore, the computational complexity scales as \\mathcal{O}(n \\cdot d^2), which necessitates aggressive dimensionality reduction to remain feasible for high-dimensional models. This framework directly overlaps with the project's Variant C, confirming the utility of spectral filtering for outlier detection.  
FLAME mitigates backdoor attacks by fusing density-based clustering, adaptive weight clipping, and Differential Privacy (DP) noise injection. The architecture represents a three-stage server-side pipeline. Initially, the HDBSCAN clustering algorithm identifies and filters models with significant angular deviations, utilizing cosine distance to operate without requiring a predefined cluster count. To limit the impact of adversaries scaling up their weights, FLAME computes an adaptive clipping bound S\_t based on the median L\_2-norm of the admitted updates. Finally, the mathematical formulation defines a DP noise boundary \\sigma\_G \\approx \\frac{S\_t}{\\epsilon} \\sqrt{2 \\ln \\frac{1.25}{\\delta}}, calculating the exact amount of Gaussian noise required to eliminate residual backdoor triggers. The primary strength of FLAME is its ability to neutralize targeted backdoors without severely degrading benign model performance. However, its reliance on geometric separability is a notable weakness; adversaries employing advanced constrain-and-scale techniques can mimic benign update distributions to bypass the HDBSCAN filter. The computational complexity is dominated by pairwise cosine distance calculations, scaling at \\mathcal{O}(n^2 \\cdot d). This methodology validates the utilization of cosine distance and weight clipping proposed in the project's Variant A.  
A critical examination of the threat landscape is provided in the literature detailing poisoning attacks against FL-based IoT IDS. This research defines an indirect attack architecture where adversaries compromise vulnerable IoT devices rather than the aggregation gateways. The compromised devices inject malicious traffic patterns, functioning as backdoor triggers, directly into the local training datasets. The anomaly detection analysis focuses on how attackers can bypass local IDS models by strictly manipulating the Poisoned Data Rate (PDR). The strength of this work lies in its demonstration of a highly practical, stealthy attack vector that evades both clustering and DP-based defenses by keeping the PDR exceptionally low. However, the attack assumes that the aggregator utilizes standard federated averaging or rudimentary distance-based clustering, leaving a gap for more advanced defense mechanisms. This specific threat model, characterized by targeted data poisoning and label-flipping via IoT nodes, perfectly aligns with the adversarial conditions intended for the current project context.  
The FREPD framework introduces an alternative approach, utilizing server-side Variational Autoencoders (VAEs) to isolate malicious updates via reconstruction error. The architecture involves training a VAE on early-round updates, which are assumed to be pristine. Subsequent client updates are scored based on the VAE's inability to accurately reconstruct them. The anomaly scoring relies on the variational lower bound of log-likelihood, expressed as $\\log p\_\\theta(x) \\ge \-D\_{KL}(q\_\\phi(z|x) |  
| p\_\\theta(z)) \+ \\mathbb{E}*{q*\\phi}\[ \\log p\_\\theta(x|z) \]$. Rather than employing a rigid threshold, FREPD utilizes the Kolmogorov-Smirnov (K-S) test to fit a probability distribution to the benign reconstruction errors. Updates exceeding the 90th percentile of this distribution are purged prior to aggregation. While FREPD effectively isolates both Byzantine and backdoor attacks, its fundamental weakness is the unsafe assumption that the initial federated rounds are entirely devoid of malicious actors. This framework heavily overlaps with Variant B of the current project, although FREPD's probabilistic distribution fit offers a more nuanced approach than raw reconstruction error thresholding.  
Further exploration of reconstruction-based defenses is found in research focused on learning to detect malicious clients through spectral anomaly detection. This architecture generates a supervised anomaly detector on the server by utilizing adversarial data compiled from historical poisoning attacks. The anomaly detection mechanism embeds high-dimensional updates into a low-dimensional latent space to precisely measure consistency and reconstruction error. The primary strength of this approach is its adaptability to ever-changing adversarial environments, provided the server has access to a comprehensive historical attack dataset. Conversely, the necessity of a clean validation dataset or extensive historical attack signatures is a significant weakness, as such data is often unavailable in zero-day IoT deployment scenarios. This methodology supports the project's integration of reconstruction errors and spectral embeddings found in Variants B and C.  
Hybrid Reputation Aggregation (HRA) is specifically designed for 5G and edge networks, merging geometric distance metrics with historical reputation tracking. The architecture computes the geometric median of incoming updates and calculates distance-based anomaly scores. The mathematical formulation updates the reputation score r\_j at round t+1 using an Exponential Moving Average (EMA): r\_j^{(t+1)} \= \\rho r\_j^{(t)} \+ (1-\\rho)\\phi(\\Delta\_j), where \\phi is a penalty function based on the Euclidean distance \\Delta\_j to the geometric median. The aggregation logic performs a weighted average using the product of the current anomaly score and the historical reputation. HRA is highly effective because it is attack-agnostic and systematically penalizes persistent adversaries over time, minimizing the impact of stealthy backdoor insertions. However, the geometric median computation requires \\mathcal{O}(I \\cdot n \\cdot d) complexity, which is computationally heavy, and the median itself can be skewed by severe Non-IID data distributions. The integration of EMA in HRA directly validates the project's momentum-based trust scoring component.  
The Trustworthy and Fair Federated Learning (TFFL) framework proposes a Dynamic Reputation Consensus (DRC) mechanism that integrates Subjective Logic and cooperative game theory. The architecture utilizes hybrid reputation assessments to establish adaptive incentive mechanisms and rigorously penalize malicious updates. The mathematical formulation employs a Subjective Logic opinion triplet consisting of trustworthiness, distrust, and uncertainty, combined with Shapley values to dynamically weight client contributions. While TFFL provides a robust mathematical foundation for fairness and contribution valuation, the computation of Shapley values is highly complex, often scaling at \\mathcal{O}(2^n) in naive implementations. This renders the approach highly impractical for resource-constrained edge deployments without significant approximation. Although mathematically distinct from the current project, TFFL underscores the academic demand for persistent reputation tracking systems to counter advanced persistent threats in FL.  
The FedLAW framework transforms the robust aggregation process into a joint optimization problem, learning the aggregation weights concurrently with the global model parameters. The architecture employs an alternating minimization algorithm where the server first minimizes the objective with respect to the aggregation weights, followed by the model parameters. The mathematical formulation updates the weights via proximal mapping onto a sparse unit-capped simplex: w\_{k+1} \= \\text{prox}\_{\\Delta\_{t, l\_0}^+}(h\_k), where h\_k \= w\_k \- \\beta \\nabla\_w \\Phi\_k(w\_k). The simplex ensures that the sum of weights equals one (\\sum w\_i \= 1\) and restricts maximum influence (w\_i \\le t), effectively pushing the weights of detected adversarial updates exactly to zero. FedLAW successfully adapts to label imbalances in Non-IID settings and mathematically guarantees the exclusion of severe outliers. However, the requirement of two communication rounds per training epoch increases the communication overhead, presenting a weakness in bandwidth-constrained IoT networks. This framework directly overlaps with the Simplex Weighting optimization step proposed in the current project architecture.  
Addressing the challenge of high dimensionality, research on layerwise aggregation introduces a method to improve (\\alpha, f)-Byzantine resilience. The architecture decomposes neural network updates layer-by-layer, substituting Euclidean distance with cosine distance, and applying median gradient clipping. The mathematical formulation proves (\\alpha, f)-Byzantine resilience, ensuring the expected aggregate deviates by no more than angle \\alpha from the true gradient: \\langle \\mathbb{E}F\_1, g\_1 \\rangle \\ge (1 \- \\sin \\alpha\_h)||g\_1||^2. The approach drastically improves accuracy in high-dimensional Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) by preventing parameter imbalances across disparate layers from distorting global geometric scoring. However, layerwise processing multiplies the aggregation loop overhead by the total number of layers in the model, increasing server-side latency. This research fundamentally validates the project's hypothesis regarding final-layer cosine similarity, proving that cosine distance is vastly superior to Euclidean metrics in sparse, high-dimensional spaces.  
Finally, SpectralKrum fuses historical spectral subspace estimation with the geometric neighbor selection of the Krum algorithm. The architecture constructs a trimmed Principal Component Analysis (PCA) subspace utilizing a rolling buffer of historical aggregates. Incoming client updates are projected into this low-dimensional space, where Krum is subsequently applied. The mathematical formulation calculates the orthogonal energy of an update d outside the benign subspace as \\rho(d) \= ||d \- U U^\\top d||\_2. SpectralKrum is exceptionally strong against subspace-aware adversaries, such as adaptive-steer attacks, that attempt to mimic benign spectral signatures. Conversely, the framework exhibits a significant weakness against label-flipping attacks; because label-flip vectors remain spectrally indistinguishable from benign updates, SpectralKrum oscillates and fails to outperform simple statistical baselines. This highlights the limitations of pure spectral defenses and shares conceptual overlap with the project's Variant C.

## **Novelty Gap Analysis**

An objective and stringent analysis of the proposed architecture reveals distinct delineations between solved problems, overused academic tropes, and genuinely defensible novelty. The reliance on standard Euclidean distance for anomaly detection in federated learning is fundamentally obsolete. The curse of dimensionality renders Euclidean metrics ineffective for high-dimensional neural network weights, and classical frameworks such as standard Krum and Bulyan have been mathematically proven to fail under Non-IID distributions. Furthermore, standard robust statistics, including Trimmed Mean and Median, are easily circumvented by adversaries who distribute minor perturbations across correlated coordinates.  
The academic landscape is currently saturated with generic "Trust-based FL" architectures. Appending an Exponential Moving Average (EMA) to a standard anomaly score and branding it a novel "Reputation System" is ubiquitous, as evidenced by HRA. Similarly, utilizing Differential Privacy (DP) noise to dilute backdoor attacks, a core mechanism of FLAME , is an overused technique that creates an inescapable and widely documented trade-off between security and global model utility. Finally, deploying PCA or SVD to locate malicious subspaces, as seen in DnC , is a standard procedure and no longer constitutes a standalone research contribution.  
Consequently, several potential novelty claims within the current project context are highly unsafe. Claiming the introduction of a server-side Autoencoder to detect malicious updates is indefensible; FREPD and FLDetector have thoroughly exhausted the server-side VAE paradigm. Asserting novelty by projecting weights onto a Capped Simplex is equally unsafe, as FedLAW has already introduced proximal mapping onto a sparse unit-capped simplex for FL aggregation. Lastly, claiming historical reputation tracking as a novel contribution ignores the extensive literature surrounding HRA and TFFL.  
The genuinely defensible novelty in the proposed architecture lies at the intersection of decoupled final-layer specialist scoring, optimization-based computational efficiency, and realistic Non-IID IoT deployments. By evaluating only the final classification layer of a PyTorch Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP), the dimensionality is drastically reduced from the full parameter space d to merely the number of classes c. While layerwise aggregation processes all layers independently, targeting exclusively the final layer for anomaly scoring while aggregating the remaining layers standardly is a highly novel approach to the "Non-IID vs. Malicious" dilemma. Early feature-extraction layers in Non-IID IoT edge nodes exhibit massive, legitimate variance due to localized traffic patterns. By ignoring these early layers and calculating Cosine similarity or SVD strictly on the final mapping layer, the server avoids falsely penalizing valid Non-IID data.  
Coupling this final-layer score with a Capped Simplex projection presents a highly defensible contribution. While FedLAW utilizes a simplex, applying this continuous optimization specifically to decoupled final-layer Autoencoder or Spectral scores to instantly force severe attacker weights to exactly zero—bypassing the latency of \\mathcal{O}(K^2) distance matrices entirely—is a robust, computationally elegant solution tailored for IoT gateways.  
A rigorous simulated review from top-tier journals such as IEEE TDSC or IEEE IoT Journal will immediately challenge the final-layer assumption. A reviewer will inevitably question what prevents an adversary from leaving the final layer pristine while poisoning the early feature-extraction layers to implant deep feature backdoors. The defense against this critique must be deeply rooted in the architecture of lightweight MLPs used for tabular IoT traffic, such as the CIC-IDS2017 dataset. In these shallow networks, an effective backdoor must ultimately map to the final output logits. If the final layer weights are strictly constrained by the benign consensus via simplex projection, malicious early-layer representations cannot successfully trigger the target class without altering the final layer's decision boundaries, which the scoring mechanism will immediately detect. SpectralKrum's documented failure against label-flipping is perfectly resolved by this final-layer scoring, as label-flipping exclusively manifests as extreme variance in the final classification layer.

## **Pipeline Design Exploration**

Leveraging insights from the comprehensive literature review, ten distinct candidate architectures have been formulated. These pipelines adhere to strict operational constraints: they rely on lightweight PyTorch MLP clients operating within the Flower framework, assume populations of 20 to 50 clients, prohibit heavy client-side computation, and strictly avoid the use of Large Language Models (LLMs) or Graph Neural Networks (GNNs).  
The first candidate pipeline is the Final-Layer Cosine with Simplex Optimization. The data flow begins with clients transmitting their MLP weights to the Flower server. The server decouples and extracts only the final layer, computes pairwise Cosine Similarity among all clients, and generates a Median Absolute Deviation (MAD) outlier score. This score is projected onto a unit-capped simplex, generating weights for the final aggregation. The computational complexity is \\mathcal{O}(K^2 \\cdot c), where c represents the class count. This pipeline is mathematically sound and perfectly addresses label-flipping, though its \\mathcal{O}(K^2) scaling limits its application in environments with massive client counts. The novelty is moderate, as cosine distance is established , but final-layer isolation is rare.  
The second candidate is the Final-Layer Autoencoder with Simplex Projection. The server extracts the final layer and processes it through a dedicated Server-Side Autoencoder. The resulting Reconstruction Error serves as the anomaly score, which is subsequently passed to the Simplex Projection for weighting. The complexity is highly efficient at \\mathcal{O}(K \\cdot d\_{AE}). This architecture scales linearly with clients and is adept at catching highly non-linear parameter corruption. However, the critical weakness is the necessity for clean warm-up rounds to train the Autoencoder, violating assumptions of zero-day readiness.  
The third architecture explores Final-Layer Truncated SVD with Simplex. After extracting the final layer, the server stacks the vectors into a continuous matrix and executes Truncated SVD. Clients are scored based on their projection variance away from the principal component, followed by Simplex Projection. The complexity rests at \\mathcal{O}(K \\cdot c^2). This method perfectly captures subspace deviations , but remains highly vulnerable to adaptive attackers who mathematically align their malicious updates with the principal component.  
The fourth pipeline introduces Cascaded Layerwise Decoupling. The server calculates Cosine similarity for every layer of the MLP independently, computes an average anomaly score across all layers, processes this through an EMA momentum tracker, and finalizes with Simplex projection. The complexity balloons to \\mathcal{O}(L \\cdot K^2 \\cdot d\_l). While this approach guarantees the detection of deep feature backdoors , it blatantly violates the efficiency constraint necessary for IoT gateways by multiplying the latency across all network layers.  
The fifth candidate proposes Dual-Stream Profiling. The final layer is evaluated simultaneously for directional shift using Cosine Similarity and magnitude explosion using the MAD of the L\_2 norm. The discrete scores are summed, fed into the EMA, and projected via the Simplex. Complexity remains at \\mathcal{O}(K^2 \\cdot c). This pipeline directly counters adversaries who scale their weights up to bypass angular checks. However, it introduces redundancy, as the Simplex projection natively limits the impact of magnitude scaling.  
The sixth pipeline designs a Momentum-Driven Historical SVD. The server maintains an EMA of the historical benign update matrices rather than relying on current-round calculations. SVD is performed on this historical subspace, and incoming updates are projected onto it before Simplex weighting. The complexity is \\mathcal{O}(K \\cdot c^2). This approach mimics SpectralKrum and is highly resilient to subspace-aware attacks. Its primary weakness is a sluggish adaptation to sudden, legitimate concept drift frequently observed in active IoT networks.  
The seventh candidate involves Federated Knowledge Distillation on the Final Layer. The server maintains a highly curated, small public validation dataset. Client final layers are utilized to generate output logits on this data, and the distributions are compared via KL-Divergence to generate anomaly scores for Simplex projection. The complexity is \\mathcal{O}(K \\cdot |D\_{val}|). Output-behavioral scoring is historically superior to weight-based scoring, but this architecture violates a strict constraint: the server cannot possess or maintain a raw validation dataset.  
The eighth architecture is Progressive Triage Layer Scoring. The server initially evaluates only the Final Layer via fast Cosine Similarity. If the MAD score exceeds a predetermined soft threshold, the server unpacks the penultimate layer specifically for that suspicious client to run a secondary Autoencoder verification. The complexity is an efficient \\mathcal{O}(K \\cdot c) \+ \\mathcal{O}(K\_{suspicious} \\cdot d\_{penultimate}). This acts as a highly efficient triage system mirroring real-world network IDS, but proves highly complex to implement smoothly within Flower's standard aggregate\_fit strategy API.  
The ninth candidate explores SpectralKrum strictly on the Final Layer. The final layer is extracted and projected into a PCA subspace. The Krum distance algorithm operates exclusively within this low-dimensional space before passing the output to the Simplex. Complexity is \\mathcal{O}(K^2 \\cdot r), where r is the PCA rank. This combines the strengths of spectral and geometric defenses , but suffers from Krum's inherent inability to process dispersed, legitimate Non-IID updates, even when restricted to the final layer.  
The tenth pipeline proposes a Meta-Trust Ensembler. The server executes Cosine, Autoencoder, and SVD scoring simultaneously on the final layer. These three scalar scores are passed into a server-side Meta-MLP trained to output a definitive anomaly score for the Simplex. Complexity sits at \\mathcal{O}(K^2 \\cdot c). While this represents the ultimate ensemble defense, the Meta-model requires a vast labeled dataset of "malicious" versus "benign" weight matrices to train effectively. This is academically indefensible in a real-world unsupervised FL deployment.

## **Hybrid Designs to Investigate**

A deeper investigation into hybrid architectural concepts is required to validate their feasibility for the final pipeline. The concept of Weighted Ensemble Scoring (A) attempts to combine Cosine, AE, and Spectral scores into a single CombinedTrustScore using a static formulation such as Score \= w\_1(Cos) \+ w\_2(AE) \+ w\_3(SVD). This approach is mathematically flawed for unsupervised settings. Determining the optimal static weights w\_i is impossible without an external validation dataset to measure performance. Furthermore, if the SVD component flags a false positive due to extreme Non-IID data distributions, it degrades the highly accurate Cosine score, pulling the entire ensemble down. The computational complexity also balloons to encompass all three operations for every client, blatantly violating IoT efficiency goals.  
Adaptive Scorer Selection (B) proposes that the server calculates the variance of updates, utilizing the AE if variance is high, or Cosine if variance is low. This logic is entirely circular. In order to ascertain whether a specific attack is occurring, and to analyze its characteristics or variance, the server must first mathematically score the updates. By the time the attack characteristics are identified to switch the scorer, the scoring operation has already been executed. This renders the adaptive switching computationally wasteful and operationally illogical.  
The Meta-trust Architecture (C) suggests training a server-side Meta-MLP to learn optimal weights for Trust\_A, Trust\_B, and Trust\_C. The risk of overfitting in this scenario approaches absolute certainty. A neural network trained to output a trust score requires a comprehensive, labeled dataset distinguishing "malicious" from "benign" weight matrices. FL fundamentally assumes zero visibility into the true nature of updates, rendering this approach academically indefensible without assuming the existence of a public, perfectly labeled root dataset.  
Multi-Stage Filtering (D) introduces a highly practical approach where Stage 1 filters obvious outliers via a computationally cheap \\mathcal{O}(K^2 \\cdot c) Cosine operation. Stage 2 subsequently verifies only borderline or suspicious clients using a more intensive \\mathcal{O}(K \\cdot c^2) SVD or AE process. This methodology mirrors real-world intrusion detection triage systems. The complexity is strictly bounded by the cheapest operation for the vast majority of benign clients, representing a substantial structural and practical novelty in FL aggregation pipelines.  
An Attack-Aware Defense (E) attempts to detect the probable attack type—such as Label Flipping versus Backdoors—before activating the most suitable scorer. The prevailing literature explicitly demonstrates that classifying the specific intent of an attack directly from high-dimensional weight gradients is significantly more difficult than merely detecting an anomaly. This adds an unnecessary classification bottleneck that is guaranteed to fail against zero-day perturbations or hybrid adversarial strategies.  
The Final-Layer Specialist Architecture (F) decouples the final classification layer (W\_{final}) from the remainder of the model parameters (W\_{model}), applying anomaly detection exclusively to W\_{final}. This is the most viable and defensible innovation investigated. In MLP architectures designed for tabular IoT data, early layers map raw numerical features, such as packet size or inter-arrival time, into a latent space. The variance in these early layers across Non-IID IoT edge devices is massive, yet entirely legitimate. The final layer is solely responsible for mapping this latent space to fixed network classes. If an attacker executes label-flipping or data poisoning, the mapping to the specific target class in the final layer must physically alter to minimize the local cross-entropy loss. Therefore, the final layer contains the highest concentration of malicious gradients and the lowest concentration of legitimate Non-IID variance. This methodology is mathematically sufficient and operationally vastly superior to full-model operations.

## **Experiment Design**

To rigorously validate the defensible novelty of Final-Layer Decoupling combined with Simplex Optimization, the experiment design must be uncompromising and technically exhaustive. The core of the evaluation will rely on extensive ablation studies. The absolute baseline will utilize standard FedAvg. Ablation 1 will implement Full-Model Cosine paired with Simplex, designed to empirically prove that full-model scoring fails catastrophically on Non-IID data by falsely rejecting benign clients. Ablation 2 will implement Final-Layer Cosine paired with standard Averaging, entirely removing the Simplex to prove that continuous optimization bounds are mathematically necessary to cap extreme magnitude attacks. The final proposed model will test Final-Layer Cosine paired with Simplex.  
Non-IID sensitivity studies are critical for this domain. The CIC-IDS2017 dataset will be partitioned using a Dirichlet distribution to simulate real-world IoT heterogeneity. The framework must be tested against extreme heterogeneity (\\alpha \= 0.1, \\alpha \= 0.5) and compared against moderate (\\alpha \= 1.0) and IID (\\alpha \= 100\) environments. The primary hypothesis is that full-model geometric defenses, such as Krum or Bulyan, will collapse entirely at \\alpha=0.1, while the proposed Final-Layer scoring will maintain a high true positive rate of benign client acceptance.  
Attacker ratio benchmarks will test the system's robustness at 10\\%, 30\\%, and 45\\% Byzantine ratios. Operating over a 50\\% malicious ratio is theoretically impossible for purely unsupervised robust aggregation, and this boundary must be respected in the simulation. Runtime and complexity benchmarks will measure the server-side aggregation time in seconds per round, scaling the client population from K=10 up to K=100. This will definitively prove that \\mathcal{O}(K^2 \\cdot c) Final-layer processing is exponentially faster and less memory-intensive than \\mathcal{O}(K^2 \\cdot d) full-model operations, verifying its suitability for IoT gateways.  
The primary metrics evaluated will include the Global Model F1-Score to represent Main Task Accuracy, the Attack Success Rate (ASR) to quantify backdoor efficacy, and the False Positive Rate (FPR) of benign client rejection. To ensure rigorous scientific validity, statistical significance testing will be employed. A paired t-test executed over five independent random seeds will be utilized to prove that the F1-score improvement of the proposed architecture over state-of-the-art baselines like HRA, Krum, and Trimmed Mean is statistically significant, maintaining a p-value strictly below 0.05.

## **Final Recommendation**

Based on the synthesis of the literature, architectural gap analysis, and strict adherence to IoT deployment constraints, the top five candidate pipelines are ranked as follows:

1. **Multi-Stage Final-Layer Triage (Pipeline D \+ F):** Combines final-layer specialization with a fast cosine filter and a rigorous SVD secondary check, culminating in a Simplex projection.  
2. **Final-Layer Cosine \+ Simplex (Pipeline 1):** Fast, mathematically sound against label-flipping, and highly publishable, though slightly less novel structurally than a multi-stage approach.  
3. **Momentum-Driven SVD (Pipeline 6):** Adapts SpectralKrum logic to historical EMA. Highly effective against backdoors, but struggles slightly with IoT concept drift.  
4. **Progressive Layer Scoring (Pipeline 8):** Extremely efficient in resource triage, but technically complex to implement within the standard PyTorch and Flower serialization APIs.  
5. **Cascaded Decoupling (Pipeline 4):** A safe approach heavily supported by Layerwise Cosine literature, but ultimately lacks the computational efficiency required for edge servers.

The definitive recommendation is the **Multi-Stage Final-Layer Triage** pipeline. This architecture provides the absolute optimal trade-off between deployment feasibility, implementation complexity, and genuinely defensible academic novelty.  
This pipeline offers vast improvements over the contemporary literature. Compared to HRA , which relies on a geometric median that collapses when Non-IID data creates multiple legitimate clusters, the proposed pipeline scores only the final layer. This removes the vast feature-space variance that shatters geometric medians, providing a pristine signal for anomaly detection. Unlike FREPD , which requires a VAE trained on pristine, guaranteed-clean early rounds, the Multi-Stage Triage requires zero warm-up rounds. Cosine and SVD operate dynamically on the geometric reality of the current round, ensuring resilience to zero-day attacks from round one. Compared to FedLAW , which requires two heavy communication rounds per epoch to compute learnable weights via loss gradients, the proposed pipeline computes the simplex projection purely through server-side weight analytics, requiring only standard single-round communication. Furthermore, while Layerwise Cosine evaluates every single layer, wasting processing cycles, the proposed pipeline recognizes that the final layer is the absolute mathematical bottleneck for label-flipping and backdoor mapping, achieving equivalent security for a fraction of the computational cost. Finally, it solves SpectralKrum's critical failure against label-flipping; by restricting SVD verification strictly to the final layer, the label-flip becomes the dominant spectral anomaly, allowing the algorithm to instantly flag it rather than absorbing it as standard variance.

### **Implementation Roadmap**

The implementation roadmap necessitates extending the flwr.server.strategy.Strategy class by overriding the aggregate\_fit method. The first technical step involves Decoupling; utilizing PyTorch state\_dict keys (e.g., fc3.weight, fc3.bias), the server extracts only the final layer from the deserialized numpy arrays, flattening them into 1D vectors per client.  
For Stage 1, the system will implement scipy.spatial.distance.cdist to execute highly optimized pairwise cosine distance matrices. The server will calculate column-wise medians and the Median Absolute Deviation to assign initial Z-scores. For Stage 2, vectors flagged as suspicious will be stacked, and numpy.linalg.svd with full\_matrices=False will be executed. The scalar projection of these suspicious vectors onto the first right-singular vector will finalize their anomaly score.  
The server will maintain a persistent Python dictionary mapping each client\_id to its historical Trust score, applying the EMA update formulation. The system will then implement an \\mathcal{O}(K \\log K) sorting algorithm to execute the Capped Simplex projection, converting continuous trust scores into strictly bounded aggregation weights. Finally, the server will multiply these output simplex weights against the entirety of the original client model parameters, summing them to produce and broadcast the secured global model for the subsequent federated round.
